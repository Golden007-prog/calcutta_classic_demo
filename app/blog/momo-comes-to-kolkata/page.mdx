# How the momo conquered the para

The momo is not from Kolkata. This is the first thing to admit, and the last
time it matters.

It came down from the Himalayas in the pockets of Tibetan families who settled
in the city in the **1960s**, bringing with them a dumpling that asked very
little: flour, filling, steam, patience. Kolkata — a city that has never once
refused a snack on principle — took approximately one winter to adopt it.

## The localisation project

What happened next would horrify a purist and delight everyone else:

- The gentle tomato achar of the mountains was replaced by a **red chutney
  with a criminal record**.
- The filling got louder — more ginger, more garlic, an amount of onion that
  means business.
- Someone, somewhere, dropped one in hot oil. The fried momo was born, and
  nobody apologised.

By the 1990s the momo had done what only the roll and the phuchka had managed
before it: become **para infrastructure**. A neighbourhood without a momo
steamer was considered underdeveloped, whatever the municipal records said.

## The 5 PM phenomenon

Watch any Kolkata momo stall at five in the evening — ours included, opposite
Deshbandhu Park — and you'll see the whole city compressed into one queue:
school kids betting pocket money, office-returners negotiating one last plate
before dinner, uncles who claim they're "just walking past" for the fourth
consecutive day.

> Steam rises at noon. By evening, it's a public service.

The momo conquered the para not by being foreign or fancy, but by being
exactly what this city wanted: hot, immediate, shareable, and priced for
everyone. Sixty rupees. [Come have a plate.](/menu/steam-momo)
